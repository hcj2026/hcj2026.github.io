Annise Parker (candidate):

Harris County government is about law enforcement. It's about public health. It's about flood prevention. It's about traffic and transportation. And it's about making it possible for people to live here safely.

Two-term Houston mayor Annise Parker off the sideline and back in the political fray, offering her leadership as the next Harris County judge. Parker said she's confident of winning the Democratic primary next March and pledged an administration focused on the fundamentals including law enforcement, flood mitigation, and government integrity.

Annise Parker (candidate):

There were no scandals on my watch when I was in office; there will be no scandals on my watch when I'm Harris County judge. I can't do anything about what's happening now; I can only say look at my record. Wait for me to get there, and I will do you proud.

Oh yeah I think it's something that she's wanted to do and decided it was time to come home and do so. The rumors are that Hidalgo's not going to run, but it's something I think she's wanted to do for a long time, and she's come home to do so.

What's your take on Annise, and it's midsummer before the primary, she jumped out there?

It's funny one of the Tea Party's often talks about how when she was first running for mayor she came and spoke at their event because of how fiscally conservative she was. But what I will say is that the progressive commentary surrounding Annise's entry has been really interesting. There's been a lot of criticism over her support of [former Harris County District Attorney] Kim Ogg. There has been criticism over her fiscal conservatism from some of the left-wing of the party, so it's going to be interesting to see how dominant they are in this primary. Is the same coalition that pushed back against Ogg's re-election going to come out support a more progressive candidate in this race? Or is that old-school blue-dog Democrat voter going to come out and turn out for Annise Parker?
