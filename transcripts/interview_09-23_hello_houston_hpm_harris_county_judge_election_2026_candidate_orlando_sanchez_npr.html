Harris County Judge 2026

Orlando Sanchez on Running for Harris County Judge

September 23, 2025

Transcript

Ernie Manouse (host):

Joining us on the phone right now is Orlando Sanchez. Orlando served for six years on Houston's city council and was elected as treasurer for Harris County for twelve years, and now as of this morning just about an hour and ten minutes ago, he's running for Harris County judge. Orlando welcome to Hello Houston.

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Good morning Celeste, Frank, and Ernie.

Frank Billingsley (co-host):

Hello Orlando.

Celeste Diaz Schurman (co-host):

Hi welcome to Hello Houston.

F. Billingsley (co-host):

So you're throwing your hat into the ring. Guts or glory?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well as we say in Texas ain't my first rodeo.

F. Billingsley (co-host):

No so why do you want this particular corral I'd like to know?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

You know Frank I have served in a non-partisan position at the city of Houston. Actually I was the first Hispanic immigrant ever elected citywide in Houston, and then I had the honor of serving twelve years, overseeing the investment and the expenditures of the county as County Treasurer, and when I was County Treasurer our books were balanced; we were reducing our debt; we had money at the end of every fiscal years; and as all of you know today Harris County is $275 million dollars in debt and growing.

F. Billingsley (co-host):

I think Annise Parker would make the same argument with her experience, and I think that's at least one of the contenders you're going to be facing from the Democratic Party, and you're going to run I guess as a Republican. How do you meet her head-on?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well Annise and I were deskmates for four years as city council members. She has similar experience; she was the city controller while I was the county treasurer. Of course Annise went on to be mayor. I lost the mayoral contest in 2001 very narrowly when I ran against Lee Brown, so our management philosophies are similar. Here's the issue. Harris County is now a plurality Hispanic community. We're the largest community in the United States with the largest number of immigrants, and I tell people all the time what does Hidalgo, Ramirez, Gonzalez, Garcia, and Briones all have in common? They're all Hispanic, all elected countywide or in their respective precincts, so the county is shifting over to a more Hispanic participation in government, and so it'll be a very lively contested debate for the position of county judge. I can tell you I have a lot of respect for Annise Parker. We've been friends for years before either one of us in public life. I knew her when she was in the private sector. She knew me when I got started in politics, so the citizens of the Harris County voting population are going to have to decide the race between Annise Parker and Orlando Sanchez.

C. Diaz Schurman (co-host):

Orlando given the fact that you just said talking about this shift within Harris County, how are you going to basically move forward with one of the issues that you have, which is I will begin the elimination of mandatory DEI training and left-wing employment practices on day 1, and that is from your website basically saying you're going to end wasteful practices. How do you reconcile those two things given that we are such a diverse area and county?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well Celeste you know that I started the largest Hispanic political action committee in Texas. I've been responsible for electing dozens and dozens of young conservatives across the state of Texas. We've won two national awards for our polling, and just on Tuesday in Austin at the Texas Public Policy Foundation we released the results of our 2025 poll. Hispanics are not aligned with a lot of the very left-leaning positions in other words open borders, defunding the police, too much consideration for ethnic groups; in other words Hispanics are focused on the economy, access to healthcare, improving the life of their families, and they're not so much worried about these policies of DEI, which by the way are scraping a lot of money out of the county funds to fund these departments.

The Hispanic community and all communities in Harris County want public safety number one. I was at Crime Stoppers for their annual remembrance murder victims. We had 700 victims murdered in Harris County between 2024 and 2025, and so that's what we want to focus on. We have limited tax dollars, so we have to address infrastructure, flooding. We've got to quit raiding the toll road authority, and we've got to expand our jail. We have expanded our courtrooms which means more adjudications, more people going into the jail, but we have no room, so people want to focus on core issues, not DEI, not social engineering. That's not a priority for Harris County residents.

E. Manouse (host):

Orlando I'm curious the decision to make this run, how was the thought process for you? When did you come to the decision to do it, and what was it that pushed you toward it? Was it simply the fact that you looked at the surnames of these folks, and you said this is the time for a Hispanic to make that move? What were you thinking?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well perhaps. Every candidate looks at numbers, right? We have to figure out is there a path to victory? What is your path to victory? But Ernie you know that ever since I've been in politics I've had the support of the Hispanic community in Houston. When I ran for mayor of Houston in 2001 the Hispanic portion of the total vote was seven percent. We doubled that to 14 percent, and I got 74 percent of the Latino vote against Mayor Lee Brown. In 2014, 2015 when I was county treasurer, [Harris County Judge] Ed Emmett and I were the highest votegetters in Harris County. We beat the Lieutenant Governor, the Governor, candidates running for Senate, and so I have a history of attracting and encouraging crossover vote from Latinos that we know historically have voted with the Democrats.

E. Manouse (host):

But I guess a little broader though besides just simply that you looked and it looked like you could win, what enticed you that this is the office for you that you thought you know what these are the issues I need to address, of course the fiscal responsibility portion of it, but what else and why now take this choice?

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well the fiscal responsibility's the most important part. For the last 15 years I've been overseeing billions, billions of dollars of public money and private sector money. I've been on the board of directors for a bank for 18 years. I was the investment chair and the chairman of the foundation for the University of Houston. We manage over half a billion dollars for the benefit of the colleges and student scholarships. I'm on the board of the largest hospital corporation in America, HCA hospital which has many hospitals here in Houston, so the number one driver for me was fiscal responsibility, but beyond that Ernie, if you drive the city of Houston, and you see the massive number of homeless people, we have some important issues to address with mental health in our community. We need to give access to healthcare to those that are mentally challenged. The county jail is not the position for them. In other words we can't have the county jail serving the mentally challenged in our community. The raiding of the Harris County Toll Road Authority and paying salaries in excess of $450 million, that's more than the President of the United States makes, is unacceptable, so we need to reign in the out of control spending, scrub the budget, address public safety, expand our courts, expand our jail, expand access to the healthcare particularly for the mentally challenged, prepare and make sure we have enough reserves to handle all the storms that may come our way, hurricanes, floods, tornadoes, you name it; we have to be prepared.

E. Manouse (host):

Let me just ask this then, in light of what you've just said and your knowledge of finance and balancing and stuff, what did you learn from your campaign when you ran for Houston city controller against Chris Hollins, and what lessons from that will help you in this campaign.

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Well let me tell you there were several candidates in the race, among them some conservative candidates, not the least of which was a sitting city council member from Kingwood. We eliminated him, and Chris and I went in to a runoff. From a purely campaign perspective, Chris outraised me by ten-to-one. Chris had about $2.2 million dollars to dump into his campaign, and I had about $250,000 dollars, so I was severely overspent, but in spite of that I recevied over 70,000 votes in the city of Houston, so that leads me to believe that I continue to have good name identification, not only in the city of Houston but in Harris County, and every candidate that runs for office looks for an advantage, and one of the advantages I have of course is not only having served honorably in all of my positions, but I have high name recognitiion.

F. Billingsley (co-host):

Orlando I've always admired how some mayors and county judges really get along and work together in the past, which is not something I've seen recently, so you as a Republican, John Whitmire as a Democrat, how are you going to work that relationship.

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

Extremely well, I've known John Whitmire ever since his early days in the [Texas State Senate]. In fact he's my neighbor, lives five blocks from me. I live in Rice Military. I won't of course disclose where the mayor is for obvious reasons. I see the mayor at Memorial Park when we jog. We've sat together at the Houston Livestock show and Rodeo. I've worked with him in the [Texas State Legislature] before. I've been to his office, presented him issues that were facing Harris County. John is interested in the wellbeing and I say that not disrespectly, but Mayor Whitmire has had the interest of Houston and Harris County for many years. He was a leader in the state legislature when it was time to build prisons. All of you remember that in the 1980s during the Tracy G murder and the Peña and Earhart murders, when gangs were running out of country, the Southwest Cholos, it was John Whitmire who led the charge to expand the prison system in the state of Texas so that we wouldn't have to parole violent offenders that were coming to Houston, so I think John Whitmire and myself are aligned on the criminal justice issues. As you all know I served twelve years as a member of the bail bond board of Harris County. When defendents were held accountable and actually showed up to court because several people had skin in the game. That is if they didn't show up to court the bail bondsmen lost money, and the family of the defendent lost the money. Today we have some courts over a 90 percent no-show rate.

E. Manouse (host):

Orlando I'm sure we're going to be hearing from you a lot more over the coming months. This race isn't going to totally be decided for quite a while yet, so it's going to be an interesting time. Good luck and thank you for sharing with us so soon after you're announced.

Orlando Sanchez (candidate):

It's great to be with the icons of Houston thank you.

E. Manouse (host):

Well then we won't keep you from them any longer.

Frank Billingsley (co-host):

We should mention that Marty Lancton a Republican and Letitia Plummer a Democrat also have their hat in the ring.

E. Manouse (host):

Thank you, that is Orlando Sanchez who has just announced that he is running for Harris County judge. Of course that name is familiar to you; he served six years on Houston City Council before elected to treasurer of Harris County for twelve years, and his now running for Harris County judge.
